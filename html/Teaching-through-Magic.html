Teaching through magic

To enhance the memorability of a speech is one way of introducing principles with the use of magic but the practicality of it might make you question the effort needed to achieve it. What would be the gain though if the purpose of speech was exactly to teach your audience a lesson and at the same time creatively influence their way of seing the subject. Through knowledge of magics and the intermingling of psychology you could achieve a set goal by using the students' untapped subconcious brain power without them even knowing it. That would make the goal an interesting riddle to solve rather than a subject of boring work. Many illusions may help clients practice cognitive skills, such as following complex directions,sequencing, memory, conceptualization and problem solving. Furthermore, illusions tend to involve colors, numbers, alphabet/reading or mathematics.

The spellbook of the wizzard-lecturer

The wizzard lecturer needs vast array of knowledge and wisdom to perform. One of the magic tools that his spellbook should include is Mental forcing. It involves giving an audience the impression that they have a free choice from all aforementioned picks on a subject, but actually use some technique to expose them to a correct answer for longer than the other choices, thereby influencing their decision. The audience is then put under pressure to answer quickly but, at the same time the free choice is still emphasised. In fact the ‚Äòfree choice‚Äô was no such thing at all [2]. Through the process the audience will gain self confidence while the wizzard-lecturer prepares to unfold a hard critical thinking problem which requires higher participation. This method ensures that no chewed answers are provided but with guidance, the prooning of wrong information is done subtly and participation is achieved.

Here is a small demonstration of how it works and how it could work for you, by changing the words and the numbers to fit your subject.

  • Think of a number from 1 to 10.
  • Multiply that number by 9.
  • If the number is a 2-digit number, add the digits together.
  • Now subtract 5.
  • Determine which letter in the alphabet corresponds to the number you ended up with (example: 1=a, 2=b, 3=c, e.t.c.)
  • Think of a country that starts with that letter.
  • Remember the last letter of the name of that country.
  • Think of the name of an animal that starts with that letter.
  • Remember the last letter in the name of that animal.
  • Think of the name of a fruit that starts with that letter.
